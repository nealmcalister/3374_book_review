The first two chapters “A Peasant’s Kingdom” and “For Love or Money” ascribe shifting economic effects to the emergence of Web 2.0. Taylor’s goal is to show the distinct negative sides of the Web, since it now occupies nearly every household. Web 2.0. revolutionized communication, and transformed business. In these chapters, Taylor enumerates her grievances about the way the digital shift commercializes the population.

Taylor begins by discussing Internet giants such as Facebook and Google, who profit immensely off of user information. The information is collected using algorithms that track users’ habits and interests. These companies sell this data to advertising companies, who in turn profit off of users, their content creators. Taylor informs readers of their susceptibility to becoming “products,” categorized based on content of one's own online searches. Participants are susceptible largely due to the illusion of how “open people’s lives are,” and this directly contributes to how “easily they can be tracked and exploited by private interests” (33).

Internet giants’ ability to profitize and control digital culture monopolizes the market, which is “tilted in favor of the most massive players” (46). She explains that the invisible hand of their authority effects all markets including film and music. This causes a decline in creative culture, because content generators are not receiving proper credit and compensation. Instead, users are “sharecropping”—creating “content” to distribute amongst the entire digital sphere (26).

In the second chapter, Taylor references Laura Poitras, a journalist who engaged in a three-year quest to create her perfect documentary entitled The Oath, only to realize her efforts had been overshadowed by technically superior amateurs in her industry. She mentions Pointras’ struggle to create her elaborate content, persuading readers to sympathize with creators who endure technological obstacles. Pointras’ creative struggle alludes to Taylor’s personal perspective towards digital artist disadvantage. She purports that “[t]he arts do not benefit from technological advancement in the way other industries do” (61). This artist disadvantage, denoted to “productivity lag,” while manufactured goods decrease, creative goods continue to increase since these products can’t be technically mass produce, they require human ingenuity.

Taylor reveals that with the Internet’s convenience, the value of their creativity is diminished, and therefore reduces artists’ profit. She says that the creative work is supposed to be shared, but notes that the problem is “how to support this kind of work in a market-based society” (70). The text also covers the economic downfall of artists who are the main content creators on the web. These artists’ free work is now being sought as leisure, instead of “legitimate labor.”
