The People's Platform

Taking Back Power and Culture in the Digital Age

A Collaborative Book Review


Introduction Chapters 1 & 2 Chapters 3 & 4 Chapters 5 & 6 Conclusion Works Cited

Astra Taylor’s The People’s Platform: Taking Back Power and Culture in the Digital Age suggests alternative ideas for achieving a sustainable digital culture. Following the digital evolution of the emergence of Web 2.0, corruption of Internet giants, artist disadvantage, and environmental concerns. Taylor’s plan for democratization emerges, intending “to invent new ways of sustaining and managing the Internet to put people before profit” (248). The text’s purpose is to inform her audience that “[a]dvertising-financed culture is not free,” it is actually extremely costly, inflicting expenditures ranging from currency to morale (260).

Web 2.0 added a virtual world to the physical, deceiving the American digital culture into believing in a more renowned self and balanced economy. Internet giants are the new corporations monopolizing their occupants, centralized around advertising needs while giving the illusion of a free market, allowing for constant privacy infringement on users to increase advertisers sales. Through copyright and Americans newfound need for instant information, artists are left to either sell out or starve, due to exploitation and elimination of in-depth investigatory trades. This virtual world has blocked Americans from seeing the devastating effects of its digital culture, emphasizing constant product production and use.

Through revealing these truths to the masses and joining together to break down digital monopolies, Americans can become a more sustainable economy, allowing individuals to truly envision what the Internet initially promised. By copyright law revision, artist and audience appreciation, and the production of environmentally sensible technologies Taylor believes Americans can reach the sustainability.

Taylor’s text provides ample use of jargon and reason, sufficiently providing the content needed to understand her argument in relation to each theme. Appealing to artist need for digital culture revision, Taylor tends to provide solutions in relation to their pessimistic adversary. Without artist incentive they won't produce creative goods, or if digital product use continues to rise at its current rate we won’t have the energy to perpetuate the culture they supposedly guarantee.
