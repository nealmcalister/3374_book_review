The People's Platform

Taking Back Power and Culture in the Digital Age

A Collaborative Book Review

In the chapters titled “What We Want” and “Unequal Uptake,” Taylor discusses assumptions maintained by 21st century “digital natives” that the Internet provides users with heaps of information. This in turn allows Internet to provide equality of hierarchical power.

In chapter three, “What We Want,” Taylor argues that the Internet giants—the few individuals who maintain control over the masses—have elicited incredible amounts of information. This information can be produced at record speeds and employed by users even more quickly for a digital-media-consumed culture. Publishing information on the Internet has become a race between news corporations and bloggers of who can release information faster. Who can produce more quickly? This race has fooled digital natives into believing that quickly-produced information is content-worthy information—that the rate of production outweighs the quality of production.

The text argues that this act of churning out news stories like factory work has made it next to impossible for deep journalism to prevail. Therefore, this digital-media-consumed culture is left with only bite-sized, watered down chunks of information, while journalists, unable to produce well-researched, well-written stories at lightning-fast speeds, are incapable of competing with the Internet leaders of news stories and updates. The effects of the rapid consumption of information by digital natives wreak havoc upon deep journalism by reducing the need for it. Digital natives begin searching for massive production rather than substantial content.

In chapter four, “Unequal Uptake,” Taylor counter-argues that the Internet provides a safe haven from inequality by describing how the Internet redistributes the hierarchical power already found in society. She asserts that politics still play a major role in the realm of the Internet by creating hierarchies which project power onto those fortunate enough to be able to participate in the digital world. Most people, Taylor informs, are still not able to connect to the Internet at the frequency of privileged individuals. This in and of itself creates a hierarchy which digital enthusiasts choose to ignore, blinding users to the utilization of their content as a product to be bought or sold. By ignoring this hierarchy, digital enthusiasts engage in the unequal balance of the digital world, creating for themselves a platform above minorities. This platform silences minorities in the digital world—minorities, whose voices will never be heard.

Taylor suggests that digital media creates new hierarchies and that it has taken advantage of deep journalism. Taylor beckons these new subcultures to look at the systems digital media giants have created. While bringing up many valid problems digital media has wrought upon culture regarding hierarchies and deep journalism, Taylor offers little solutions to these problems. Although she offers remedies to digital-media problems in her conclusion, these two chapters might seem more complete if Taylor would have incorporated a few ideas for solving the problems on which she places so much importance. Taylor could have, for example, created a problem-solution theme within chapters three and four, which would have generated a stronger tone of urgency.

If the reader was able to read Taylor’s progressive solutions directly after reading Taylor’s analysis of 21st century digital problems, perhaps he or she would feel more inclined to take action.
