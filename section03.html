Taylor’s concluding chapters “The Double Anchor” and “Drawing the Line” examine the adverse design of copyright, which she believes discourages artists to create while encouraging Internet giants to dominate digital culture. She attributes this domination in part to the ideological flaws of free culture activism. Noting the primary belief that “if something is free, that means it has been de-commodified” (226), she concludes her book with a call to action for the revision of copyright laws, democratization of American culture, and the production of sustainable electronics.

Using her personal experience as a documentarian, Taylor provides a connection to copyright wars with relation to her philosophical film Examined Life, which was uploaded for free consumption before the theatrical release. In an attempt to surpass copyright, Taylor reached out to the uploaders asking for her film to be taken down for a few months, explaining her need for revenue to replenish her production deficit, to which they replied, “[p]hilosophy is free” (175). This comment being easily seen as a form of free culture activism demonstrates its sole strategy of violating copyright through file-sharing. Thus, this experience provides her with a new understanding of Jem Cohen’s “double anchor” metaphor: there is a necessity for mutual support between the creator and receiver of works (229).

Unlike the ideologies of free culture activism, Taylor believes the underlying and more detrimental intentions of Internet giants have allowed advertising companies to take over the Internet.

Taylor illustrates the hypocrisy of the Internet giants’ design. They dispense “free” products out to users while simultaneously collecting their personal information—a problem free culture activists fail to recognize as they inadvertently feed and support these giants. In relation, Taylor asserts Internet giants’ “want nothing more than their message to spread,” ergo, the free culture movement “unintentionally benefits the public relations industry” by spreading its content inadvertently while intending to dismantle (242).

Taylor’s conclusion refers to America as a commercialized world, to which we are all just targeted consumers. She asks readers to democratize in order to take down Internet giants through the fortification of fair use and de minimis, material that is so minor or insignificant that is doesn’t warrant justification for copyright infringement. In addition, she calls for the reduction of copyright time (especially for private monopolies that continuously renew), and the allowance of copyright holders to pay fees to prevent remixing. With a progressive policy favoring the creativity of individuals works based on importance rather than vitality, this policy would allow for the de-commodification of Americans, shifting our “paradox of value” from a quantitative view of creative individuals assimilated by the limitlessness of their web presence, back to a qualitative view. Lastly, Taylor asks the reader to focus on eco-sufficiency by reevaluating his or her exchange rate for products “designed to dump.” Products made to do “less damage to the environment are more durable and repairable,” and “less harmful to . . . those who will live long after” their screens go “forever black” (250).

The text provides ample information about issues surrounding technology, a product Americans tend to use daily, but know little about. Providing examples many audience members, such as students and teachers, who have been evolving with such technologies and their associated concerns for years. Many of these audience members have never been knowingly fazed by their influence, but will be left with multiple technological concerns by the end of Taylor’s book. Her multiple examples completely modify the way one views technology. These examples uncover all the little secrets technology has been trying to hide from its user.
